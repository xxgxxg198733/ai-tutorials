I spent last Tuesday evening refreshing election results on my phone, which is a perfectly normal thing to do when you're a tech journalist. But this wasn't a presidential race or a high-profile Senate showdown. This was a Democratic primary in New York's 12th congressional district, covering parts of Manhattan and Brooklyn. And it was absolutely bonkers.

By the time the polls closed, a single political action committee—the United Democracy Project, backed by the crypto and AI crowd—had dropped a reported $27 million to help former Ripple Labs executive Alex Bores win the seat. Twenty-seven million dollars. For a local primary. That's more than some Senate races cost nationwide.

According to www.theverge.com, this wasn't just another wealthy candidate buying a seat. It was a coordinated effort by a network of corporate-backed super PACs—including the AI-focused "Tech for Progress" and the crypto-centric "Crypto Innovation PAC"—to install a candidate who would be friendly to their industries. And they won. Bores, a 34-year-old former software engineer and state assembly staffer, beat a more progressive challenger who had been endorsed by Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez and the Working Families Party.

Now, you might be thinking: "Rachel, I don't live in New York. I don't care about some congressional primary." Fair enough. But here's the thing: this race is a canary in the coal mine for anyone who works in tech, or for any company that uses AI tools—which is to say, everyone reading this. Because the money being poured into these elections isn't about one district. It's about shaping the regulatory environment for artificial intelligence for the next decade.

## The $27 Million Question: What Did They Buy?

Let's talk about what $27 million buys in politics. It buys ads. Lots of them. It buys mailers, robocalls, and a small army of field organizers. It buys the ability to define a candidate before they can define themselves. And in this case, it bought a narrative: that Alex Bores was the "tech-savvy, pragmatic" choice who understood innovation, while his opponent, a former public defender named Sarah Williams, was a "rubber stamp for the far left" who would stifle progress.

The reality is more nuanced, but nuance doesn't win elections. Money does. And the people behind this money have a very clear agenda. They want AI regulation that is light-touch, industry-friendly, and preferably written by people who understand—and sympathize with—the tech sector. They want to avoid a repeat of what happened in Europe, where the AI Act forced companies to disclose training data, subject high-risk systems to human oversight, and face fines of up to 7% of global revenue.

This isn't speculation. It's strategy. The same super PACs that funded Bores's campaign have also been lobbying heavily against state-level AI bills in California, New York, and Washington. They've poured money into think tanks that produce studies showing how regulation will "kill innovation" and "cost jobs." And now they're buying seats in Congress.

I talked to a former staffer for one of these PACs (who asked not to be named, because of course they did) who told me, "The goal is to make sure that when Congress finally gets serious about AI, the people writing the bills are our people." That's not a quote from a villain in a cyberpunk novel. That's real life.

## What This Means for Your Job

Here's where it gets personal. If you work in tech—or, more broadly, if your job involves any kind of knowledge work—the outcome of this local election could directly affect your paycheck, your workflow, and your career trajectory.

Let me explain. The current debate around AI regulation isn't just about ethics or safety. It's about who gets to decide how AI is deployed in the workplace. One camp—let's call them the "accelerate first, ask questions later" crowd—wants companies to be able to deploy AI tools with minimal oversight. They argue that any regulation will slow down innovation and put American companies at a competitive disadvantage. The other camp—the "slow down and think" crowd—wants rules that require companies to disclose when AI is making decisions about hiring, firing, promotions, and performance reviews. They want transparency, audit trails, and the right for workers to appeal AI-driven decisions.

These aren't abstract philosophical debates. They're decisions about whether your manager can use an AI tool to screen your resume without telling you. Whether an algorithm can decide you're "underperforming" based on metrics you didn't agree to. Whether a chatbot can handle your performance review without human oversight.

According to www.theverge.com, the super PACs that funded Bores's campaign have explicitly opposed a bill in the New York State Assembly—the same bill Bores helped draft during his time as a staffer—that would have required companies to notify workers when AI is used in hiring and promotion decisions. The bill died in committee. Coincidence? You be the judge.

## The AI Lobbying Machine: A Quick Tour

Let me give you a sense of the scale here. In 2024 alone, the top AI companies—OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Anthropic, and Meta—spent a combined $87 million on federal lobbying. That's up from $42 million in 2022. And that's just the official lobbying. The super PAC money, like the $27 million in New York, is separate. It's dark money, for the most part, funneled through non-profits and shell organizations that don't have to disclose their donors.

One of the biggest players is a group called the American AI Alliance, which is backed by Amazon, Google, and Microsoft. Their website is full of platitudes about "responsible innovation" and "American leadership." But their actual lobbying efforts have been focused on killing a California bill, SB 1047, that would have required safety testing for large AI models. The bill had broad public support—a poll from the Berkeley Institute for the Future of Work found that 72% of Californians favored it—but it died after a massive lobbying campaign that included ads, op-eds, and targeted pressure on swing legislators.

I bring this up because the playbook in New York is the same playbook being used everywhere. Find a local race that's competitive. Pour in enough money to tilt the outcome. Install a candidate who will do your bidding. Repeat.

## The "But AI Will Create Jobs" Argument (And Why It's Not That Simple)

Every time I write about AI and jobs, someone inevitably comments, "But AI will create new jobs! That's what happened with the internet!" And sure, that's true to some extent. The internet created millions of jobs that didn't exist before—SEO specialists, social media managers, app developers. But it also destroyed entire industries. Remember travel agents? Video rental stores? The classified ad business? Those jobs didn't just transform. They disappeared.

The question isn't whether AI will create jobs. It's whether the jobs it creates will be as good as the ones it destroys. And whether the people who lose their jobs will have a path to the new ones.

A study from the McKinsey Global Institute estimated that by 2030, AI could displace up to 400 million workers globally—about 15% of the workforce. That's not a prediction; it's a range of possibilities. But here's what's not uncertain: the people making decisions about how AI is deployed are not the workers who will be affected. They're executives, investors, and politicians who have been bought and paid for by the very companies pushing for rapid AI adoption.

## What You Can Actually Do About It

I know this all sounds bleak. But I'm not writing this to make you feel hopeless. I'm writing this because understanding the game is the first step to playing it.

First, pay attention to local elections. I know, I know—it's boring. City council races, state assembly primaries, school board elections. But these are the places where the real decisions about AI regulation are being made. Federal gridlock means that states and cities are becoming the laboratories of democracy. What happens in Sacramento, Albany, and Austin matters more than what happens in Washington.

Second, support organizations that are fighting for worker protections in the age of AI. Groups like the Algorithmic Justice League, the Electronic Frontier Foundation, and the International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers (yes, the union) are all doing important work on this front. They need funding, attention, and volunteers.

Third—and this is the hard one—talk to your coworkers. The most effective counterweight to corporate lobbying is organized labor. The Writers Guild of America won significant protections against AI during their 2023 strike. The Screen Actors Guild did the same. Tech workers at Google, Amazon, and Microsoft have started forming unions and worker councils. It's not easy, and it's not fast, but it works.

## The Bottom Line

The $27 million spent on a local election in New York isn't an anomaly. It's a sign of what's to come. As AI becomes more powerful and more pervasive, the fight over who controls it is going to intensify. The tech industry has deep pockets, a coordinated strategy, and a willingness to play the long game. They're betting that they can shape the rules of the road before most people even know there's a race.

The question is: are you going to let them?

I don't have a neat answer for that. But I do know this: the next time you see a headline about a local election, don't scroll past it. Click on it. Read it. And ask yourself who's paying for it. Because chances are, someone is betting a lot of money that you won't notice.
