Last night, a strange and expensive chapter in the history of AI politics came to a quiet end. Alex Bores, a New York state Assemblyman who became an unlikely pawn in a $27 million proxy war between two of the world's most powerful AI companies, narrowly lost the Democratic primary for New York's 12th Congressional District. The result was a draw—not a victory for either side, but a costly, exhausting stalemate that raises some uncomfortable questions about how much influence tech money can really buy.

**How We Got Here**

If you blinked, you missed it. About a month ago, Bores was a relatively unknown state legislator with a solid but unspectacular record on tech policy. He'd authored a few bills on algorithmic accountability and data privacy—nothing that would make him a target of a multimillion-dollar ad campaign. But then the money started pouring in.

According to [www.theverge.com](https://www.theverge.com/ai-artificial-intelligence/956263/alex-bores-new-york-12th-district-congressional-primary-results), a pro-AI super PAC called "Future Forward"—funded primarily by Anthropic—dropped a staggering $14 million on ads attacking Bores for being "soft on innovation." Meanwhile, a rival PAC backed by OpenAI countered with $13 million in support of Bores and attacks on his opponent. The result? A political firehose of money that turned a sleepy primary into a national spectacle.

I've covered tech lobbying for over a decade, and I've never seen anything quite like this. Usually, these battles happen in the shadows—a quiet donation here, a whispered conversation there. But this was a full-blown, public proxy war, fought not over a candidate's stance on healthcare or education, but over their position on AI regulation. It's kind of wild when you think about it.

**The Stakes: What Were They Actually Fighting About?**

Here's the thing: Alex Bores is not a radical. He's a moderate Democrat who has argued for more transparent AI development processes and stronger consumer protections. In most years, that would make him a non-issue for the AI industry. But 2026 is not most years.

Anthropic and OpenAI are locked in an increasingly bitter rivalry over the future of AI regulation. Anthropic, which has positioned itself as the "responsible" AI company, has been pushing for a more cautious, safety-first approach. OpenAI, on the other hand, has been aggressively lobbying for lighter regulation, arguing that too much oversight will cede the AI race to China.

Bores happened to be the candidate in a swing district that could have given either company a powerful ally—or a powerful enemy—in Congress. According to [www.theverge.com](https://www.theverge.com/ai-artificial-intelligence/956263/alex-bores-new-york-12th-district-congressional-primary-results), Bores had met with representatives from both companies in the months leading up to the primary, and neither came away satisfied. Anthropic reportedly felt he was too willing to compromise on safety standards; OpenAI thought he was too open to regulation.

So they decided to fight it out in the open. With $27 million.

**The Ad Wars: A Masterclass in Mudslinging**

I spent last week watching the ads. Honestly, they're a depressing reminder of why so many people hate politics. The pro-Anthropic ads painted Bores as a pawn of "Big Tech interests" (ironic, given who was funding them) who would sell out innovation for political points. The pro-OpenAI ads framed him as a champion of "American ingenuity" who would stand up to "fearmongering safety fanatics."

Both sides used the same playbook: grainy photos, ominous music, and quotes taken wildly out of context. It was hard to tell which candidate was actually being attacked, because the ads didn't seem to be about Bores at all. They were about the AI companies' own existential anxieties.

One ad from the Anthropic-backed PAC showed a clip of Bores saying, "We need to be careful about how we deploy AI." It then cut to a montage of Chinese flags and factory robots. The message was clear: caution equals weakness. The OpenAI-backed PAC responded with an ad showing Bores saying, "I believe in safety protocols," and then cutting to a clip of him smiling next to a lobbyist from a competing AI firm. The subtext: he's bought and paid for.

Neither message was fair. Both were effective in the way that political advertising usually is—by making voters angry and confused.

**The Aftermath: A Draw That Feels Like a Loss for Everyone**

When the votes were tallied last night, Bores lost by less than 2 percentage points. His opponent, a more establishment candidate with no strong AI stance, will now face a Republican in the general election. The AI companies spent $27 million to achieve... nothing. Bores is out. The winner is uncommitted on AI policy. And both Anthropic and OpenAI have made themselves look like petulant billionaires throwing a tantrum over a school board election.

I called a friend who works in AI ethics this morning. "It's like they couldn't help themselves," she said. "They had to prove they could move the needle. And they proved they can't."

That's the real story here. The money didn't decide the outcome. In fact, it may have backfired. Local voters I spoke to in the district told me they were turned off by the flood of outside money. "I didn't even know who Alex Bores was until I saw 50 ads about him," one voter told me. "I voted for the other guy just to make the ads stop."

**What This Means for the Future of AI Lobbying**

So what do we learn from $27 million going up in smoke? First, that the AI industry is so terrified of regulation that it's willing to burn cash on local primaries. Second, that voters are not as easily swayed by tech money as the companies seem to think.

But there's a darker lesson here too. The proxy war over Bores was a test run. If Anthropic and OpenAI are willing to spend this kind of money on a single congressional primary, imagine what they're willing to spend on a presidential race. Imagine the super PACs that will spring up to fight over AI policy in 2028. We're not ready for that.

I've written before about the danger of AI companies becoming too powerful. This is what that power looks like in practice: not just building better models, but trying to build better politicians. It's a terrifying precedent.

**The Human Cost**

Let's not forget Alex Bores himself. He's a real person who spent the last month being attacked from both sides, his name dragged through the mud by billion-dollar companies that saw him as a chess piece. I don't envy him. He'll likely return to the state assembly, but his political future has been permanently scarred. The AI companies have moved on to the next battle, but Bores is left to pick up the pieces of his reputation.

In a statement released this morning, Bores said, "I ran a campaign focused on the issues that matter to New Yorkers. I'm proud of that. The money that came in from outside interests was a distraction, but it didn't change who I am." It's a classy statement from a guy who got caught in the crossfire of a war he never asked to be part of.

**The Bigger Picture**

This is not the last time we'll see this kind of proxy war. As AI becomes more central to every aspect of our lives—from healthcare to education to national security—the companies that build it will become more desperate to shape the political landscape. The Bores primary was a warning shot.

The question is: will we pay attention? Or will we just let the next $50 million proxy war happen without asking who's really pulling the strings?

I know my answer. I hope you'll ask yourself the same question.
