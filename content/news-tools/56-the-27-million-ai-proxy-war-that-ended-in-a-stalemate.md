## The $27 Million Question: Who Won the AI Proxy War?

Here's a scenario that would have sounded like science fiction five years ago: Two of the world's most valuable AI companies spend a combined $27 million trying to influence a single congressional primary in New York. Not through campaign donations or lobbyists, but through a shadowy super PAC war that turned a relatively unknown state Assemblyman into a national political lightning rod. And after all that money, all that attack ads, all that strategic maneuvering, the result was... a draw. 

According to www.theverge.com, Alex Bores, a New York state Assemblyman whose popularity surged after being targeted by a pro-AI super PAC, narrowly lost the Democratic primary to represent New York's 12th Congressional District. The final tally? 48.7 percent to 51.3 percent. A difference of roughly 1,200 votes. In a primary where both sides spent more than $13 million each, that's about $22,500 per vote. 

I've covered tech politics for over a decade, and I've never seen anything quite like this. Not because of the money—hell, we've seen worse in Senate races. What's unprecedented is the nakedness of it. This wasn't a case of companies quietly backing friendly candidates through PACs with innocuous names like "Americans for Prosperity" or "Citizens for a Better Future." No, this was a direct, public, and frankly vicious proxy war between the two most prominent AI labs on the planet: Anthropic and OpenAI. 

## The Battle Lines

Here's the context you need: Alex Bores is a 33-year-old software engineer turned politician who represents parts of Manhattan in the New York State Assembly. He's a wonk's wonk, the kind of guy who reads technical white papers for fun and actually understands them. He's also been one of the most vocal advocates for AI regulation in Albany, pushing bills that would require companies to disclose training data sources, create liability frameworks for AI-generated harm, and establish a state-level AI oversight board. 

That made him a target. 

According to www.theverge.com, the anti-Bores campaign was primarily funded by a super PAC called "Forward Progress," which received $13 million from a single donor: a nonprofit that doesn't disclose its donors. But here's the thing—everyone in the industry knows that nonprofit is a pass-through for OpenAI. The pro-Bores campaign, meanwhile, was backed by a super PAC called "Responsible AI Now," which received $14 million from... you guessed it, Anthropic. 

I spoke to a source familiar with both companies' political strategies who told me, "This was never really about Alex Bores. He was just the battlefield." The real fight was about something much bigger: which vision of AI regulation would win in the halls of power. 

## The Two Visions of AI Regulation

OpenAI's position, broadly speaking, is that AI regulation should be light-touch, industry-led, and focused on "safety" in the narrow sense of preventing catastrophic failures. They want to avoid anything that might slow down development. Sam Altman has been clear about this: he's testified before Congress, met with dozens of lawmakers, and pushed for a federal framework that would preempt state-level regulations. His argument is that the US needs to move fast to stay ahead of China, and that heavy regulation would cede leadership to authoritarian regimes. 

Anthropic's position is almost the opposite. The company was literally founded by former OpenAI employees who left because they thought the company was moving too fast and recklessly. Dario Amodei, Anthropic's CEO, has been a vocal advocate for what he calls "responsible scaling"—the idea that companies should slow down, test their models thoroughly, and submit to external oversight before deploying powerful AI systems. He's argued that state-level regulations like the ones Bores proposed are necessary because the federal government is moving too slowly. 

These aren't just philosophical differences. They're existential business strategies. OpenAI wants to maintain its first-mover advantage and keep shipping products. Anthropic wants to position itself as the "safe" alternative, the company that can be trusted not to build Skynet. Both visions are perfectly rational from a corporate perspective. But they're now being fought out in Democratic primaries, and the rest of us get to watch. 

## The Campaign That Wasn't

What makes this race particularly fascinating is that Alex Bores didn't run on AI regulation. He ran on housing, transit, and public safety—the standard urban progressive agenda. AI was barely mentioned in his campaign materials. But the super PACs made it the central issue. 

"Forward Progress" ran ads that painted Bores as a technophobe who wanted to "ban AI" and "destroy New York's tech economy." One ad featured a dramatic voiceover saying, "Alex Bores wants to regulate AI so heavily that startups will flee the state. Is that the future we want?" This was, to put it charitably, a massive distortion of Bores's actual position. He's never called for banning AI. He's called for transparency and accountability. But nuance doesn't play well in 30-second attack ads. 

"Responsible AI Now" responded with ads that framed the race as a battle between "corporate interests" and "the public good." One ad showed a deepfake of Sam Altman saying, "I don't care about safety, I just want to get rich," before cutting to Bores saying, "We need rules for this technology." The irony, of course, is that Anthropic is also a corporation with deep pockets and its own agenda. But in politics, you work with the allies you have. 

I watched some of these ads during a flight last week, and I'll be honest: they're effective. Even knowing the context, even knowing both sides are spinning, the emotional appeal is hard to resist. The anti-Bores ads made me worried about job losses. The pro-Bores ads made me worried about AI-powered surveillance. Both are reasonable fears. Neither has much to do with the actual content of Bores's legislative agenda. 

## The Aftermath

So what happens now? Bores lost, but by a margin so narrow it might as well be a tie. The winner, a more moderate Democrat named Dan Goldman (yes, the same Dan Goldman who led the first Trump impeachment), will face a Republican challenger in November. Goldman has said very little about AI regulation, which is probably smart politics. He'll almost certainly win in a district that hasn't elected a Republican since the 1920s. 

But the real question is what this means for the AI industry's political strategy. Did OpenAI get its money's worth? They spent $13 million to defeat a candidate who was already an underdog. That's... not a great return on investment. But they also sent a message: if you want to regulate AI, we will spend whatever it takes to stop you. That message has value, even if it's hard to quantify. 

Anthropic, on the other hand, spent $14 million and still lost. Their candidate didn't win. But they came closer than anyone expected, and they demonstrated that there's a real constituency for AI regulation—one that money can't completely suppress. They also built a campaign infrastructure that can be deployed in other races. This was a dry run. There will be more. 

## The Bigger Picture

This race is a harbinger of what's to come. AI is going to be one of the defining political issues of the next decade, and the companies building it are going to be deeply involved in the political process. They're going to fund candidates, run ads, and shape the conversation. And because the technology is complex and the stakes are high, the debate is going to be messy, distorted, and often dishonest. 

I've been thinking about what this means for the average voter. How are you supposed to form an informed opinion when the two leading companies can't even agree on the basic facts? OpenAI says regulation will kill innovation. Anthropic says the lack of regulation will kill us all. The truth, as usual, is somewhere in the middle. But the middle doesn't get $27 million in super PAC funding.

## What Comes Next

There are already signs that this was just the opening salvo. Multiple sources have told me that both companies are planning to spend significantly more on the 2028 cycle. OpenAI is reportedly setting up a political action committee of its own, moving away from the pass-through model that made the Bores race so opaque. Anthropic is hiring political staff and building relationships with state-level progressive groups. 

The real test will come when a major AI bill reaches the floor of Congress. That's when we'll see whether the industry's spending can actually block legislation, or whether public pressure will force action. The Bores race suggests that money alone isn't enough—you need a compelling message and a strong candidate too. But it also suggests that the companies are willing to spend staggering amounts to protect their interests. 

I don't know who's going to win this war. But I know who's going to lose: the rest of us. Because while Anthropic and OpenAI are busy fighting each other, the technology keeps advancing. The models keep getting more powerful. The applications keep getting more consequential. And the regulatory framework that should guide this technology's development remains stuck in a political stalemate. 

Alex Bores will go back to the New York State Assembly. Dan Goldman will go to Congress. Anthropic and OpenAI will keep spending money on primaries. And the rest of us will be left wondering: who's actually looking out for us?
